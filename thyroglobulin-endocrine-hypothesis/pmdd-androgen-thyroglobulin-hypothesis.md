## 🦋 Insight
###
Thyroglobulin is a large protein where thyroid hormone is stored in a scaffold [1](#ref1). The subject of this "n-of-1" study presents with two thyroid nodules with euthyroid status of serum T3, T4, Reverse T3, and TSH. No clinically significant level of anti-thyroglobulin or anti-thyroid peroxidase antibodies. Despite the initial 'bland' hormonal level appearance, this subject presents with profoundly high levels of serum thyroglobulin ($554 ng/ml$). 
## 
Despite being clinically classified as having "non-toxic nodules," this subject has been experiencing persistent subclinical hyperthyroid (thyrotoxicosis) symptoms. This includes hot flashes, a constant feeling of being hot, mild tachycardia, extreme anxiety, paranoia, intense irritability, and mild hypertension. The subject also presents subjectively by describing an ongoing "rushed feeling" that feels like "being stuck in traffic all the time with constant irritability." It is hypothesized that the thyroglobulin leaks are causing dysregulated transient T3/T4 leaks not detectable on standard blood tests.

## 🩺 Endocrinologic Connection: PMDD and Increased Androgen Hypothesis

## 
It is hypothesized that these nodules are contributing directly to PMDD and steroid-like systemic effects. The subject presents with intense PMDD that is debilitating. It causes extreme anxiety, depression, fatigue, and irritability cyclically each month for at least 10 days before the onset of menses. Thyroglobulin  modulates serum levels of sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) in serum. Notably, it has been shown that SHBG levels are relatively elevated in thyrotoxicosis [2](#ref2). Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) is a disorder that causes an extreme version of pre-menstrual syndrome (PMS). It is hypothesized to be driven by steroid hormone sensitivity.

Clinical Evidence: 
- Thys-Jacobs, S., et al. (2008) measured levels of steroid hormones in PMDD subjects and found that, for both follicular and luteal phases, SHBG was markedly higher in the PMDD group  [3](#ref3).
- Deviations in SHBG concentrations have been shown to cause significant systemic effects. For example, increased SHBG levels stimulate estrogen production, which may lead to systemic symptoms such as hirsutism and forms of hynaecomastia. The subject experienced both (enlarged and lumpy breasts), and noted they correlated with the onset of the nodules about 11 years ago [4](#ref4). It is hypothesized that these altered SHBG concentrations due to the transient serum thyroglobulin leaks are creating a state of "estrogen dominance," which is driving the PMDD by increasing steroid hormone sensitivity.

## 💫 Future Directions
- Longitudinal case study tracking benign thyroid nodules with euthyroid status despite high serum thyroglobulin. It is hypothesized that the higher the serum Tg, the more drastic the SHBG deviations and resultant downstream estrogenic effects.
- Modeling the connection between serum Tg lattices and SHBG topology.
